## Where this sits in the loop

ICP is step 1 of a five-part loop, and it is the part that decides everything after it. The order is ICP → Offer & Lead Magnet → Campaign Architecture → Copy → Lead List Building. Your ICP decides which offer and lead magnet you need, which decides which campaigns you run, which decides the copy you write, which decides the data you have to go find when you build the list.

Most people treat these as five separate jobs. They are one loop. This doc is where the loop starts - so the decisions you make here ripple through all four docs after it. Take your time on it.

## Why ICP is the leverage point

Most people who fail at cold outbound fail here, not at copy. They write decent emails to the wrong people, get terrible reply rates, and decide the channel is dead. The channel was never the problem. They were broadcasting into the wrong room.

Think of it like baking. Copy is the icing. The offer is the filling. The ICP is the ingredients themselves. You can't decorate your way out of bad batter. Get the ICP right and the rest of the system gets dramatically easier. Get it wrong and no amount of clever copy saves you.

We've watched a SaaS company doing $20M selling to "any mid-market business" - spread across a dozen industries, growing slowly, decent everywhere and special nowhere. They went all-in on one vertical and scaled past $150M. Same product. Same team. Same playbook. They just stopped talking to everyone and started talking to someone. That's the leverage.

## ICP vs buyer persona

These get blended constantly. They are not the same thing.

ICP (Ideal Customer Profile) = the company you target. *"****B2B SaaS, 100–500 employees, $10–50M ARR, US/UK, running HubSpot.****"*

Buyer persona = the person inside that company. *The VP of Sales. The Head of RevOps. The CRO.*

One ICP can have several personas. If you sell sales-enablement software, the ICP is one company type, but your personas might be the CRO (economic buyer), the Head of Sales Ops (technical buyer), and the VP of Sales (the user). Each one hears a different message.

The rule for outbound: **one persona per campaign.** Don't blend. The CRO and the Head of Sales Ops have different concerns, language, and objections. Copy that tries to speak to both speaks to neither. Separate campaigns - exactly how is covered in the Campaign Architecture stage of the full Launchpad.

## The 4-layer ICP framework

Every good ICP has four layers stacked. Build top to bottom. Each layer narrows the one above it.

### Layer 1 - Firmographics (the company basics)

The objective facts. What is measurable from the outside. How to find the pattern: look at your top 10–20 customers (highest LTV, fastest close, lowest churn) and see what they cluster around.

| **Criteria** | **Example** |
| --- | --- |
| Industry / vertical | B2B SaaS, e-commerce, professional services, healthcare |
| Company size | 50–500 employees |
| Revenue range | $10M–$50M ARR |
| Location | US-based, expanding to EU |
| Company age | 3–10 years (past early stage, pre-enterprise) |
| Business model | B2B vs B2C vs B2B2C |
| Funding stage | Bootstrapped, Seed, Series A–C, PE-backed |
| Product | What they sell, ex: skincare products |

### Layer 2 - Technographics (the tools they run)

What software they already use. It reveals budget, sophistication, and intent. A company running Salesforce plus a sales-engagement tool is signaling sales maturity, budget, and an active outbound motion - which dictates how you talk to them. A company with none of that probably isn't running outbound at all, and gets a different message.

How to find it: tools like BuiltWith, Clearbit, or HG Insights expose tech-stack data that filters cleanly inside your enrichment workflow. (Reference examples, not an exhaustive list.)

| **Signal type** | **Example tools** |
| --- | --- |
| CRM | Salesforce, HubSpot, Pipedrive |
| Marketing automation | Marketo, Pardot, ActiveCampaign, Shopify |
| Sales tools | Outreach, Salesloft, the existing outbound stack |
| Analytics | Segment, Amplitude, Mixpanel |
| Competitor tools | Companies on your competitors are ripe for switching |
| Complementary tools | Companies on tools that integrate with yours adopt faster |

### Layer 3 - Buying signals (what is happening right now)

The behavioral layer, and the one most people under-build. A buying signal - a trigger - is a specific event that makes a prospect more buyable today than yesterday. This is where you find not just who fits, but who is ready. Triggers fall into three categories.

**Individual triggers - about the person.** Things the buyer did or had happen to them:

- New role, or first time in a role of this level (gold - vendor changes happen in the first 90 days)

- Promoted recently; hiring on their team

- Posting, commenting, or following relevant content; spoke on a podcast or at an event (a public POV you can reference)

- Attending an event; joined a relevant community

Why they work: they are personal. The prospect feels seen, and the timing lines up with a moment they are actually open.

**Company triggers - about the organization.** Things happening at the company level:

- Open jobs / hiring posts (active need + budget); a role that doesn't exist (no CFO → pitch fractional; no Head of RevOps → pitch your tool)

- Recently raised funding (fresh capital, vendor-shopping mode); new tech installed (adoption window for adjacent tools)

- SEC filings / annual reports; expansion, new office, or new market; M&A; partnerships; product launches; a competitor's launch in their space

- Decision-maker departure (vendor relationships in flux); running paid ads (spending on growth)

- Layoffs or headcount cuts (cost-cutting mode - an opportunity if you reduce cost); legal issues or lawsuits (compliance, security, risk tooling)

- Negative employee reviews; website redesign / migration; awards; using a competitor (switching opportunity) or a tool you integrate with; a customer lookalike

**Industry triggers - about the category.** Things moving across the whole market - trends (AI adoption, cost pressure), analyst reports that mention your category, new regulation or legislation, and world events. Industry triggers rarely carry an email alone. They make individual or company triggers feel urgent - the macro framing around the specific reason you are reaching out.

### Layer 4 - Psychographics (what they value)

The softest layer. Hardest to pull from a database, most powerful when you nail it. Read their About page, careers page, and blog. Skim the CEO's LinkedIn. Use AI to summarize values from their site at scale.

| **Indicator** | **What it tells you** | **How to find it** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Growth-mode vs optimization-mode | Different urgency, different language | Hiring velocity, leadership messaging |
| Data-driven culture | Decision style | Use of analytics tools, data-team size |
| Customer-centric | Operating priority | NPS, CS team size, reviews |
| Compliance-focused | Risk tolerance | Industry regulations, certifications |
| Remote vs office-first | Operating model | Job postings, culture pages |

## The Signal-to-Offer Map - the bridge between who and what you say

Here is the move that ties this whole playbook together, and it is the thing most operators miss.

Every offer has a specific set of public, findable data points that prove a prospect has the exact pain you solve - right now. Those same data points do two jobs at once:

- **They score the lead.** A company showing the signal is more likely to buy, so it ranks higher and gets prioritized.

- **They write the personalization.** The signal is the specific, true thing you say in the opener so the email reads like you did your homework.

Same data. Two jobs. You don't enrich "for scoring" and then enrich again "for personalization" - it is one pass, and that is what keeps your list costs sane (more on that in Lead List Building).

The trick is mapping the signal to YOUR offer. Generic triggers (funding, hiring) are fine, but the highest-converting signals are the ones tied directly to the pain your product removes:

- **A compliance SaaS** reaching a company that just got named in an FTC action or disclosed a breach. The lawsuit is the pain. "Saw the FTC filing last month - figured compliance gaps are top of mind" both scores the lead A-tier and writes the opener.

- **A building-materials supplier** reaching a developer who just filed a construction permit or won a bid. The permit is the buying window.

- **A staffing firm** reaching a company that just posted ten roles in one department.

Ask one question for your offer: **what public event means this prospect has my pain today?** That event becomes both your filter and your first line. Map it now - it sets up the next decision.

## Two campaign motions from one ICP

Your ICP work produces two distinct lists - and they become two distinct campaign types. You decide the split here; you build the structure in Campaign Architecture.

- **The evergreen cash-cow list.** Everyone who fits your ICP on firmographics, regardless of signal. Big, broad, scalable, cheap to build. Copy has to work cold because there is no signal to lean on. Lower conversion per email, but it runs forever and pays the bills. This is your baseline pipeline.

- **The signal list.** The subset showing one or more of your Signal-to-Offer triggers. Smaller, more expensive to build (more enrichment), but far higher conversion. These leads earn hyper-personalized copy and multi-touch (email + LinkedIn + a call) because the economics justify the effort.

You run both. The cash cow funds the operation; the signal list spikes your conversion rate. Don't pick one. Hand both forward to your Campaign Architecture step.

## Build YOUR ICP - the 30-minute exercise

Stop guessing. Use real data.

- Pull your top 10% of customers. Highest LTV, fastest close, lowest churn, easiest to work with, most excited about your product.

- Find 3 patterns they share. Industry? Size? Revenue band? Tech stack? Funding stage? Geography?

- Pull your bottom 5 customers. Churned fast, slow close, high support load, painful.

- Find 2 patterns they share. This is your anti-ICP - companies to avoid even when they look good on paper.

- Write your ICP in one paragraph. Example: "B2B SaaS, 100–500 employees, $10–50M ARR, Series A–B, US/UK, running Salesforce and a sales-engagement tool, recently raised OR hiring for RevOps, with a data-driven culture."

## Score your TAM - and decide what each tier gets

A list without a score treats every lead as equal. They aren't. Score every company 0–100 against your rubric:

- Right industry: +20  •  Right company size: +20  •  Right revenue range: +15

- Uses key tech: +15  •  Shows a buying signal: +10  •  Hiring for a relevant role: +10  •  In target location: +10

- This is just an example. You decide which data points and triggers get which score.

Then tier them - and the tier decides the treatment, which is the part most people skip:

- **A-tier (80–100):** perfect fit. If they also carry a signal, they go on the signal list - hyper-personalized, multi-touch.

- **B-tier (60–79):** good fit. Pursue on the evergreen list.

- **C-tier (40–59):** okay fit. Nurture.

- **D-tier (****<****40):** poor fit. Disqualify. Don't email them to feel busy.

More signals = higher score = more personalization and more channels. That is the rule connecting scoring (here) to copy (the dream email, dialed up or down by data) and to spend (you only pay to enrich the leads worth enriching).-

## Two levels of niching

"Niche down" hides two different decisions.

- **Campaign-level niching (mandatory).** Each campaign targets one ICP segment and one persona. Never blend mid-market e-commerce with enterprise SaaS in one campaign - the messaging dilutes and the data becomes unreadable. Non-negotiable.

- **Brand-level niching (optional, eventually right).** Some companies position the whole brand around one niche ("we only serve law firms"). Others stay broad at the brand level and niche at the campaign level. Both work. Brand-level niching wins once you have the case studies to dominate one space.

The usual right move: niche at the campaign level from day one, niche at the brand level once you have the proof to own it.

## The hourglass

Most successful B2B companies follow an hourglass across their life:

- **Early (broad):** experiment widely to find a niche you can win.

- **Growth (narrow):** go all-in where the experiments worked. This is where revenue compounds.

- **Scale (broad again):** expand into adjacent niches once you have dominated the first.

If you are early, don't get stuck broad. Pick a niche, prove it, expand.

## Red ocean vs blue ocean

Once you have an ICP, ask how saturated the market is. This decides how much creative work your offer and copy have to do - so it feeds straight into the next two docs.

- **Red ocean - saturated.** Every prospect gets 30 cold emails a week in your category, and competitors 2–3x your size do roughly what you do (Facebook ads for e-comm; Amazon agencies). Generic value props die here. You need a creative offer or lead magnet, a specific mechanism (the how, not just the what), sharp differentiation upfront, and strong risk reversal.

- **Blue ocean - underserved.** Few competitors emailing them, problems unsolved. Direct wins: three-sentence emails, a plain "we do X for Y without Z," fast conversion because you are often their first option.

Real example: a B2B affordable-housing certification software targeting Section 8 housing. Blue ocean - most prospects had never been emailed about the problem. Three sentences per email, direct value prop, 20% positive reply rate, $1.2M in contracts in 90 days. The niche was so underserved that complexity would have hurt.

ICP isn't just who - it is what market state you are in. That tells you immediately how hard the offer and copy have to work.

## Pain research - the core diagnostic

Before anyone writes copy, you have to understand the pain from the prospect's point of view. Answer these for every ICP:

- What pain do they actually face? (Not what your product solves - what they wake up worried about.)

- What are they doing about it now? (Workarounds, competitors, or just living with it.)

- What is the pain costing them? (Dollars, time, risk, missed opportunity.)

- Why is their current fix broken - and worse than yours?

- What do they want instead? (The end state they picture if it went away.)

- What emotional and logical win can you deliver, better than anything else on the market?

Answer these and your offer and copy practically write themselves. Skip them and you will write generic copy because you don't actually know what the prospect cares about.

How to research it fast: read Reddit threads in their industry, G2 reviews of competitors, LinkedIn posts from people in that role. Listen to podcast episodes with that persona. Have 5 real conversations. Or have Claude do the reading and surface the pattern, which is exactly what this skill does.

## Common ICP mistakes

- **Too broad.** "Any company with 10+ employees." You can't market to everyone. Specificity wins.

- **Too narrow.** "Fintech in SF with exactly 150 employees." TAM too small to sustain. Fine for a one-off, not for pipeline.

- **Built on assumptions, not data.** Talk to customers, look at your CRM, don't guess.

- **Static.** Markets move. Re-run this quarterly.

- **Ignoring the anti-ICP.** Knowing who NOT to target is as valuable as knowing who to.

- **No scoring.** Without it, every lead looks equal. Score them.

- **Treating signals as a separate, later step.** The triggers and the Signal-to-Offer Map are part of defining the ICP - they decide your two campaign motions. Do them here, not as an afterthought when you are building the list.
