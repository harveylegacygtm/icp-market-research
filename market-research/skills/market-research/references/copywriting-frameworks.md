# Research frameworks from the best in direct response

Use these to read the market before writing a word. They are the lens, not a
script.

## Market awareness (Eugene Schwartz)
Five stages of what the prospect knows:
1. Unaware: does not know they have the problem.
2. Problem-aware: feels the pain, does not know solutions exist.
3. Solution-aware: knows solutions exist, not yours.
4. Product-aware: knows yours, not sure it is right.
5. Most aware: ready, needs the deal.
Cold outbound usually lands at 2 or 3. So lead with the pain or the result,
not the product.

## Market sophistication (Eugene Schwartz)
How many times this market has been pitched:
1. First to market: a simple claim works.
2. Second in: amplify the claim.
3. Crowded: introduce a new mechanism.
4. Tired: make the mechanism more believable, or niche down.
5. Burned out: lead with identity, proof, and a sharper niche.
Cold B2B markets are usually 3 to 5. You win with a new mechanism or a tighter
niche, not a louder promise.

## Enter the conversation (Robert Collier)
Match the conversation already running in the prospect's head. Do not start a
new one. The phrasing they already use for the problem becomes your copy.

## Mass desire (Gary Halbert, the starving crowd)
You cannot create desire. You channel an existing one. Pick the market by its
hunger first, then the offer. A starving crowd beats a great burger.

## Research first (David Ogilvy, Claude Hopkins)
The homework is the work. Ogilvy: the consumer is not a moron. Hopkins: ad
writing is salesmanship in print, measured by results. Specifics, real proof,
and testing beat clever lines.

## Voice of customer
The best copy is found, not written. Mine reviews, Reddit, forums, and call
transcripts. Steal the exact words. Real quotes outsell paraphrase.
